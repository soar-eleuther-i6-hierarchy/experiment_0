Kill rates across depth — why the coverage edges die

Raw edge counts aren't comparable across layers (layer 6's B2→B3 has 4.7M candidate edges, layer 12's only 431k). The honest question is what fraction survives each metric — a normalized ratio that is comparable. Bars are proportional; darker fill = larger share.

1 · Candidate edges (raw) — the misleading magnitude

What the crude coverage test proposes. Bars are on a log scale. Big ≠ real — the next two tables show almost none survive.

2 · Improve reconstruction (%) — the "real refinement" survivors

Share of candidate edges where ablating the parent actually hurts the child's reconstruction. Higher = more real. Bars scaled to the table max (42.5%).

3 · Frequency-driven (%) — the co-occurrence artifacts

Share whose coverage collapses on rare tokens — i.e. the edge only exists because both features are common. Higher = more artifact. Bars 0–100%.

What the ratios say

Reconstruction dies with block depth

Averaged over the 5 layers: ~16.9% of B0→B1 edges improve reconstruction, dropping to ~0.5% (B1→B2) and ~0.2% (B2→B3). The pattern holds at every layer — deeper block pairs carry no reconstructive signal.

Frequency rises to near-total

Frequency-driven share climbs from ~50% (B0→B1) to ~98% (B1→B2) to ~99.9% (B2→B3) at every layer. The deep "hierarchy" is co-occurrence between common features.

B0→B1 is the only real signal — and it's non-monotonic

Reconstruction survival at B0→B1 peaks at layer 3 (42.5%), collapses at layer 6 (6.3%), and never recovers. Depth does not sharpen the hierarchy.

Raw counts mislead

Layer 6 has the most candidate edges (4.7M at B2→B3) yet the lowest survival (0.0% recon, 99.9% frequency). Magnitude tracks co-firing density, not hierarchy — which is exactly why the ratio, not the count, is the right lens.
